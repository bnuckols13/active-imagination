Imaginal Arts & Depth Practice — Process, Not Perfection

◆ Today's Intention

Today's group draws from the depth psychological tradition of active imagination — the art of allowing images to arise from within and engaging them without trying to control the outcome. Rooted in the understanding that psyche is always generating images, symbols, and felt senses that carry meaning, this practice invites us to listen rather than direct. We fuse this with a core principle: process, not perfection. What matters is not the beauty of what you create, but the encounter itself. The healing lives in the doing.

Principle I

Start with what you feel, not what you think

Principle II

Let images arrive on their own terms — don't force or choreograph them

Principle III

Stay curious rather than critical — soften the inner editor

Principle IV

Psyche has its own intelligence — treat what arises as a guest, not a possession

◈ The Imaginal — What Are We Practicing?

Active imagination is not guided imagery, daydreaming, or free association. It's a practice of encountering the images that live beneath conscious awareness — and engaging them in relationship. The depth tradition recognizes that we don't just have an unconscious; it has us. Images, symbols, and figures arise constantly — in dreams, in symptoms, in the body, in the creative impulse. Usually we ignore them, explain them away, or try to control them. Today we practice something different: we meet them.

◆ Affect as Threshold

▸

In depth work, the doorway to the imaginal is feeling, not thinking. Rather than analyzing an emotion, we sink into it — noticing its texture, weight, temperature, and location in the body — and wait for what naturally emerges. The affect is not a problem to solve. It's the threshold to cross.

The practice is deceptively simple: make yourself as conscious as possible of the mood you're in, stay with it without reserve, and notice what images, colors, shapes, or words begin to present themselves. Emotion carries the image; the image carries the meaning.

◈ Softening the Inner Critic

▸

The first practical step is creating what depth psychologists call a vacuum in consciousness — a receptive, open space where the judging mind softens and something unexpected can arrive. This is not mindlessness; it's a disciplined hospitality.

Most of us carry an inner voice that criticizes, judges, dismisses, or undermines our creative process the moment it begins. This can be understood as the ego's resistance to allowing deeper material airtime. Today we practice noticing that critic and gently setting it aside — not silencing it, but not obeying it either. We acknowledge the voice and return to the feeling.

◇ The Autonomy of Images

▸

One of the most radical ideas in depth psychology is that images have their own life. We tend to assume we generate our thoughts and images — that they belong to us, that we're their author. But the depth tradition suggests otherwise: inner figures, dream images, and spontaneous fantasies arise with an autonomy that surprises us precisely because they don't come from our conscious intentions.

As the archetypal psychologist James Hillman put it, we should attend to images on their terms rather than translating them into something our ego already understands. When we create today, we're not manufacturing — we're receiving visitors. Your task is to be a gracious host, not an architect. Let the image surprise you. Let it be strange.

◇ Two Paths: Making & Meaning

▸

The depth tradition describes two complementary ways of working with what arises from the imaginal. Creative formulation means giving the image tangible form — through painting, movement, sound, writing — and letting themes coalesce intuitively. Understanding means sitting with the material and asking what it means for your life, what it's pointing toward.

Both paths have value, and both have pitfalls. Pure formulation can become aestheticized — we get lost in making it beautiful and forget the encounter. Pure understanding can become intellectualized — we analyze the life out of the image. Today we lean into creative formulation first and understanding second, trusting that meaning often reveals itself through the making. This is the heart of "process, not perfection."

◇ Ground Rules for Today

◆ No critiquing — your own or others' work. The inner editor gets a break today.

◆ Process over product. The healing is in the encounter, not the artifact.

◆ Honor autonomy. Pass on any station. Modify freely. Your body knows what it needs.

◆ Confidentiality. What's shared in group stays in group.

◆ Stay grounded. If intensity arises, return to your senses: feet on floor, hands on surface, slow exhale. Depth work requires a grounded body.

◆ Creative Stations

Choose one or two stations that call to you. Each begins the same way: start with what you feel, soften the critic, and let the image arrive on its own terms. Spend 20–30 minutes in your chosen station. Click a station to expand its full instructions.

Remember: you can always pass or sit quietly with your experience. Stillness is its own depth practice.

Station I

The Threshold Image

Visual Arts — Paint, Draw, Collage

Begin with a felt sense and let a visual image emerge without planning the composition.

Entry: Close your eyes. Drop into the feeling that is most present for you right now. Notice its quality — is it heavy? Tight? Buzzing? Cool? Without forcing anything, wait for a color, a shape, or a scene to emerge. It may be fragmentary, half-formed, or nonsensical. That's welcome.

Engagement: Begin putting this on paper. Use whatever medium draws you — paint, pencil, torn paper, marker. Do not plan the image. Let your hand follow the feeling, not the other way around. If the image shifts, follow it. If the critic speaks, acknowledge it and return to the feeling. You are not illustrating something you already know — you are discovering what psyche wants to show you.

Dialogue: When the image feels "enough" (not finished — enough), pause. Look at it as if it were a visitor who arrived uninvited. If this image could speak, what would it say? Write one sentence from the image's perspective on the back or margin.

Provide a range of materials: watercolor, oil pastels, torn magazine images, colored pencils. Emphasize that skill is irrelevant — stick figures, abstract marks, and "ugly" images are all welcome and often carry the most energy. Watch for participants who freeze; gently suggest starting with a single color or a single mark.

Station II

Voices in the Room

Writing — Dialogue & Imaginal Encounter

Engage in written dialogue with an inner figure, feeling, or image — as if it has its own voice and perspective.

Entry: Identify a feeling, recurring thought, or inner figure that has been present lately — perhaps an anxious voice, a grieving part, a dream image, or even a body sensation with a personality. Give it a name or title. It could be as simple as "The Heavy One" or "The Watcher at the Edge."

Engagement: Begin a written conversation. Write your question or greeting, then let the figure respond. Don't script the response — write whatever comes, even if it surprises you. The depth tradition holds that inner figures are not merely aspects of "us" — they carry their own intelligence, their own agenda. You don't have to agree with them. You do have to listen. Continue the dialogue for at least one full page.

Close: Thank the figure. Ask yourself: what did they say that I didn't expect? Where did the conversation take me that my conscious mind wouldn't have chosen?

Some participants may find this station emotionally activating. Normalize that — the material arises because it's ready to be met. Remind them they can pause, ground, or move to a different station at any time. Offer the option of writing with the non-dominant hand to further bypass the critic.

Station III

The Body Knows

Movement & Gesture

Let the body express what words and images cannot — start with somatic awareness and let movement emerge.

Entry: Stand or sit comfortably. Place one hand where you notice the strongest sensation in your body. Breathe into that area. Without choreographing, let a small movement begin — a sway, a curl, a reach, a tightening. Follow it the way you'd follow a thread in the dark.

Engagement: Let the movement grow or stay small — both are valid. You are not performing; you are listening to the body's own imaginal language. Notice if the movement has a rhythm, a direction, a quality (sharp, fluid, stuck, circular). Stay with it for several minutes. If it resolves into stillness, honor that stillness as its own kind of image.

Capture: After your movement, take 2 minutes to sketch the shape or path your body traced — not a literal drawing, but an abstract gesture on paper. Or write 3 words that name the quality of the movement.

Play ambient, non-directive music or silence. Ensure enough physical space between participants. This station can be done seated for those with mobility needs. Eyes closed is encouraged but optional. The somatic dimension is central to depth work — the body is not separate from psyche.

Station IV

The Unheard Song

Sound & Voice

Explore what your inner landscape sounds like through toning, humming, rhythm, or found sound.

Entry: Close your eyes and listen inward. If your current emotional state had a sound — not a word, but a sound — what would it be? A hum, a sigh, a drumbeat, a whisper, a growl? Let it form without judgment. Psyche speaks through many channels — for some people, the auditory is the most natural doorway.

Engagement: Begin making the sound — softly at first, then at whatever volume feels right. Use your voice, tap on a surface, use available instruments. Let the sound evolve. Follow its natural arc. It may shift from discordant to melodic, or stay raw throughout. Both are honest. You are not composing — you are listening to what psyche wants to sound like.

Capture: After sounding, sit in the silence that follows. Notice what shifted in your body. Write or draw a "sound map" — marks on paper that represent the shape of what you heard from within.

Provide hand drums, singing bowls, shakers, or simple percussion. For groups where voice feels too vulnerable, rhythm-only is a comfortable entry point. Participants can also listen rather than produce sound — receptive witnessing is a valid form of imaginal engagement.

Station V

The Inner Landscape

Sculpting & Tableau

Use clay, objects, or body posture to give three-dimensional form to your inner experience.

Entry: Choose your medium — clay, found objects, or your own body posture. Hold the feeling that is most present. Ask yourself: if this feeling were a landscape, a creature, or a scene, what would it look like? Let the image come to you rather than designing it.

Engagement: Build without a blueprint. Let your hands make decisions your mind hasn't approved yet. If using objects, arrange them in relationship to each other — which one feels central? Which is at the edge? What's the distance between them? The psyche thinks in spatial relationships as much as in words.

Dialogue: Step back and look at what you've made as if encountering it for the first time. Ask: "What are you showing me?" Give the central element a voice — write one line of what it would say if it could speak.

Air-dry clay, pipe cleaners, stones, buttons, fabric scraps, and miniatures all work well. For body-based work, participants can create a frozen posture (tableau) representing the feeling. Pairs can mirror each other's postures for deeper attunement. This station draws on the depth tradition's recognition that psyche externalizes itself through arrangement and form.

Station VI

The Open Gate

Intuitive — Your Own Modality

Trust whatever channel feels most alive for you. Combine modalities, sit in stillness, or follow impulse.

Entry: The depth tradition recognizes that each person has their own primary channel of imaginal access — some see images easily, others hear words or music, others feel movement or know through the body. Rather than choosing a prescribed station, follow your own channel. What is your body-mind already doing?

Engagement: You might combine modalities — draw while humming, move and then write what the movement said, sculpt and then speak to the sculpture. Or you might sit quietly and let an image, memory, or sensation unfold internally. This, too, is active imagination. The only criterion: stay conscious and in relationship with what arises. Don't drift. Don't analyze. Attend.

Capture: However you engaged, leave a trace — a word, a mark, a gesture, a single sentence. Something to anchor the imaginal encounter when you return to ordinary awareness.

This station serves participants who feel constrained by structured options, and also those who have developed their own imaginal practice. It also functions as the "pass with presence" option — sitting quietly and attending inward is fully honored here. Receptive awareness is not passive; it's one of the deepest forms of participation.

◇ Integration & Reflection

Take a few minutes to ground yourself after your creative work. The depth tradition warns against merely letting images rise and then moving on. The real work is in realizing them in actual life — drawing meaning from what emerged and carrying it forward. Integration is where the process becomes personal.

Returning to the Body

Before reflecting, take three slow breaths. Notice what has shifted in your body since the start of group. Are you lighter? Heavier? Softer? More awake? Whatever you notice, let it be information — not judgment. The body is the first and last witness to the imaginal encounter.

Reflection Prompts

Choose one or two that resonate. You're welcome to write, share aloud, or hold your reflection silently.

Invite voluntary sharing in pairs first (2–3 minutes each), then open to the wider group. Remind participants they can share the experience without showing the artifact. Normalize all responses — including "nothing happened" or "it was uncomfortable." Both are valid data from the imaginal encounter. Resistance and emptiness carry their own messages.

◈ Discussion Seeds

For group processing after individual reflection:

◆ What was it like to create without a plan? How did your inner critic show up, and what did you do with it?

◆ The depth tradition suggests inner images have their own autonomy — they're more like visitors than inventions. Did your image surprise you or take a direction you didn't choose?

◆ Where do you notice the tension between process and product in your daily life — at work, in relationships, in how you evaluate yourself? What would it mean to soften that?

◆ What did it feel like to start with feeling rather than thinking? Was this natural, unfamiliar, or somewhere in between?
